# Rationalization Techniques — Deep Reference

## 1. Developability Analysis

A surface is developable if and only if its Gaussian curvature K is zero everywhere. Developable surfaces can be unrolled flat without stretching, tearing, or compressing — making them directly fabricable from flat sheet material (metal, plywood, composite) with pure bending.

### 1.1 Gaussian Curvature Check

**Definition**: The Gaussian curvature at a point P on a surface is K = kappa1 * kappa2, where kappa1 and kappa2 are the principal curvatures (maximum and minimum normal curvatures) at P.

**Interpretation**:
- K > 0: The surface curves in the same direction along both principal directions (bowl-shaped). Examples: sphere, ellipsoid. Not developable.
- K = 0: The surface curves in one direction but is straight in the other (cylinder-shaped), or is flat. Examples: cylinder, cone, tangent surface. Developable.
- K < 0: The surface curves in opposite directions (saddle-shaped). Examples: hyperbolic paraboloid, hyperboloid of one sheet. Not developable, but contains two families of asymptotic (straight) lines.

**Computation on a mesh**: For a triangle mesh, Gaussian curvature at a vertex v is computed via the angle defect formula:

K(v) = (2 * pi - sum of face angles at v) / A(v)

Where A(v) is the area associated with vertex v (one-third of the total area of all faces incident to v, or the Voronoi area for more accurate results).

For a quad mesh, curvature must be estimated from the dihedral angles along edges or by fitting local quadric patches (paraboloid fitting) to vertex neighborhoods.

**Threshold for developability**: In practice, K = 0 is never achieved exactly on a free-form design surface. A surface region is considered approximately developable if |K| < epsilon, where epsilon is chosen based on panel size and material flexibility. Typical threshold: |K| < 0.001 m^(-2) for panels up to 2 m, |K| < 0.0001 m^(-2) for panels up to 5 m.

### 1.2 Ruling Direction Analysis

Every developable surface is ruled — it can be generated by moving a straight line (the ruling) through space. The ruling direction at each point of a developable surface is the direction of zero normal curvature (the direction along which the surface is straight).

**Finding ruling directions on a nearly-developable surface**:
1. Compute principal curvatures (kappa1, kappa2) and principal directions (e1, e2) at each point.
2. The direction of minimum absolute curvature is the approximate ruling direction.
3. If |kappa_min| < threshold, the surface is locally ruled in that direction.

**For surfaces with K < 0 (negative Gaussian curvature)**:
There are two asymptotic directions at each point — directions along which the normal curvature is zero. These are the ruling directions of the two families of rulings on a doubly-ruled surface (e.g., the two families of straight lines on a hyperbolic paraboloid). Both asymptotic directions can be exploited for strip decomposition.

**Asymptotic direction computation**:
The asymptotic directions satisfy II(v, v) = 0, where II is the second fundamental form. In terms of the principal curvatures and directions:
- If kappa1 > 0 and kappa2 < 0 (K < 0), the asymptotic directions make angles alpha = ±arctan(sqrt(-kappa2/kappa1)) with the first principal direction e1.
- If K >= 0, there are no real asymptotic directions (except the trivial case kappa_min = 0 where the ruling direction is a principal direction).

## 2. Strip Decomposition for Free-Form Surfaces

Strip decomposition segments a free-form surface into strips, each of which is approximately developable. This is a fundamental rationalization strategy for metal cladding and other sheet-formed facades.

### 2.1 Geodesic Strip Method

1. Choose a family of geodesic curves on the surface. Geodesics are the shortest paths on the surface — they have zero geodesic curvature and follow the surface's intrinsic geometry.
2. The strips between adjacent geodesics are approximately developable if the geodesic spacing is sufficiently small relative to the surface curvature.
3. Each strip is a ruled surface: the rulings connect corresponding points on the two bounding geodesics.

**Geodesic computation**: Integrate the geodesic ODE on the surface starting from an initial point and direction. The geodesic equation in surface coordinates is:

d²u^i/ds² + Gamma^i_jk * (du^j/ds) * (du^k/ds) = 0

Where Gamma^i_jk are the Christoffel symbols of the surface metric.

In practice, Grasshopper's geodesic curve component or custom Runge-Kutta integration on a mesh suffices.

### 2.2 Principal Curvature Strip Method

1. Compute one family of lines of curvature on the surface (curves that are everywhere tangent to a principal direction).
2. The strips between adjacent lines of curvature are bounded by curves of principal curvature on two sides and cross-curves (the other family of curvature lines) on the other two sides.
3. If the cross-curvature (curvature in the strip-width direction) is approximately constant across the strip width, the strip is approximately a surface of revolution segment — which is developable.

This method works best on surfaces where one principal curvature is much larger than the other (highly anisotropic curvature), because the strips along the low-curvature direction are naturally narrow and nearly developable.

### 2.3 Approximation Error

For a strip of width w on a surface with Gaussian curvature K, the maximum deviation between the strip and its best-fit developable surface is approximately:

delta_max ≈ (K * w^3) / 24

This formula (derived from Taylor expansion of the surface normal variation across the strip) guides strip width selection:

| Gaussian Curvature K (m^-2) | Max Strip Width for delta < 2mm | Max Strip Width for delta < 5mm |
|-----------------------------|--------------------------------|--------------------------------|
| 0.001 | 364 mm | 490 mm |
| 0.005 | 215 mm | 290 mm |
| 0.01 | 171 mm | 230 mm |
| 0.05 | 100 mm | 134 mm |
| 0.1 | 79 mm | 107 mm |

On highly curved surfaces (K > 0.05), the strip width required for acceptable deviation becomes impractically narrow, and alternative strategies (cold bending, hot forming, or panelization into planar triangles) should be considered.

## 3. Conical Mesh Theory

Conical meshes were formalized by Helmut Pottmann and colleagues at TU Wien as a geometric framework for architectural freeform structures. The theory provides the mathematical foundation for designing quad meshes that are simultaneously planar-faced, torsion-free at nodes, and admit constant-width offset layers.

### 3.1 Definition

A quad mesh is conical if, at every interior vertex, the four face planes meeting at that vertex are tangent to a common right circular cone. Equivalently, the face normals at each vertex lie on a circle on the Gauss sphere (unit sphere of normal directions).

### 3.2 Relationship to Conjugate Curve Networks

A conical mesh is a discrete analog of a conjugate curve network on a smooth surface. The mesh edges follow (approximately) conjugate directions, which ensures face planarity. The additional conical condition (normals on a cone) corresponds to the mesh being aligned with the principal curvature directions of the underlying smooth surface.

In fact, the class of conical meshes is intermediate between:
- PQ meshes (planar quadrilateral meshes) — the broadest class, faces are planar but nodes may be torsion-twisted.
- Circular meshes — the most restrictive class, where all faces have circumscribed circles. Every circular mesh is conical, but not vice versa.

### 3.3 Practical Significance

**Torsion-free nodes**: At a conical mesh vertex, the four edge directions and the four face normals are related by a consistent geometric structure. The axis of the tangent cone at each vertex defines a natural "node axis." Mullion profiles placed along edges meet at the node without twisting — the intersection geometry at each node is planar. This means:
- Standard extruded mullion profiles (T, I, or custom) can be used without warping
- Node connectors (spider fittings, cruciform plates) lie in a single plane
- Welding and bolting at nodes is dramatically simplified

**Constant-width offset**: Given a conical mesh M, there exists a parallel mesh M' at a constant face-to-face distance d from M. This means:
- The outer glass layer and inner insulation layer can be modeled as parallel conical meshes
- The cavity between them has uniform depth — critical for IGU spacing, insulation thickness, and maintenance access
- Bracket lengths are uniform, simplifying procurement

### 3.4 Optimization Procedure

**Energy functional for conical mesh optimization**:

E_total = w_conical * E_conical + w_proximity * E_proximity + w_fairness * E_fairness + w_planarity * E_planarity

Where:
- E_conical = sum over all interior vertices v of ||n1 + n3 - n2 - n4||^2, where n1-n4 are the normals of the four faces around v (numbered alternately). For a conical mesh, opposite normals sum to equal vectors.
- E_proximity = sum over all vertices v of ||v - closest point on design surface||^2
- E_fairness = sum over all edges of (edge_length - average_edge_length)^2 + sum over all faces of (area - average_area)^2
- E_planarity = sum over all faces of (planarity_deviation)^2

**Solver**: L-BFGS quasi-Newton optimizer, operating on all vertex positions simultaneously. Typical problem size: 5,000-50,000 vertices (15,000-150,000 DOF). Convergence in 50-200 iterations. Total solve time: 5-60 seconds on modern hardware.

**Weight selection**: Start with w_proximity = 1.0, w_planarity = 0.1, w_conical = 0.01, w_fairness = 0.01. Gradually increase w_conical and w_planarity over iterations (continuation method) to avoid local minima.

## 4. Circular and Conic Section Structures

### 4.1 Circular Arc Mullions

Straight mullion segments on a coarse mesh produce a faceted appearance. Replacing straight segments with circular arcs produces a smoother visual appearance with fewer elements.

**Properties of circular arc structures**:
- Circular arcs can be fabricated by three-roll bending of extruded profiles — a standard and economical process
- A circular arc is defined by three parameters: radius, subtended angle, and orientation
- At each node, the tangent directions of meeting arcs determine the node geometry
- If arcs meet tangent-continuously, the structure appears smooth; if they meet at a kink, the faceted character is preserved at nodes

**Design strategy**:
1. Mesh the surface coarsely (1/3 to 1/2 the panel count of a flat-panel mesh)
2. Replace each straight edge with a circular arc that matches the surface curvature at the edge midpoint
3. Optimize arc radii to minimize deviation from the design surface
4. Check structural behavior: arcs in compression act as arches (efficient); arcs in tension may require thicker profiles

### 4.2 Conic Section Curves

Generalizing from circular arcs, any conic section (ellipse, parabola, hyperbola) can be used as a structural curve. Elliptical arcs provide gentler curvature variation than circular arcs for the same span. Parabolic arcs are optimal for uniform gravity loading.

## 5. Geodesic Line Networks

Geodesic lines (shortest paths on the surface) have a special property: when a strip of material is laid along a geodesic, it lies flat against the surface without buckling or stretching. This makes geodesic lines ideal for:
- **Strip glazing**: Glass or metal strips following geodesic paths lie naturally on the surface
- **Cable paths**: Geodesic cables on a tensioned surface follow the shortest path and therefore carry load most efficiently
- **Structural ribs**: Geodesic ribs trace the most direct load path across a curved surface

**Geodesic network construction**:
1. Select seed points on the surface boundary or at design-significant locations.
2. Shoot geodesics from seed points in specified initial directions.
3. Build a network of intersecting geodesics.
4. The network defines panel boundaries.

**Challenge**: Geodesic networks are not generally conjugate — the panels they define are not necessarily planar. Geodesic networks must be combined with planarization or triangulation to produce constructible panels.

**Exceptions**: On surfaces of constant Gaussian curvature (spheres, pseudospheres), geodesics have special regularity properties that can be exploited for uniform panelization.

## 6. Asymptotic Line Networks on Negative Curvature Surfaces

On surfaces with negative Gaussian curvature (K < 0) — saddles, hyperboloids, minimal surfaces — there exist two families of asymptotic lines: curves along which the normal curvature is zero. These lines have remarkable properties for facade construction:

### 6.1 Properties

- Along an asymptotic line, the surface is locally straight (zero normal curvature). A flat strip of material placed along an asymptotic line bends only in its own plane — it does not need to twist.
- The two families of asymptotic lines form a network. Quad panels bounded by asymptotic lines have the property that opposite edges are parallel in the tangent plane — they approximate planar parallelograms.
- The asymptotic network on a minimal surface (K = constant < 0, such as a catenoid or helicoid) is particularly regular.

### 6.2 Construction

1. Compute the asymptotic directions at a dense grid of points (see Section 1.2 above).
2. Integrate the asymptotic direction field to produce two families of asymptotic curves.
3. The intersection network defines quad panels that are approximately planar (due to the zero-curvature property along both edge families).
4. Panels may need additional planarization optimization, but the starting point is far better than an arbitrary UV subdivision.

### 6.3 Structural Implications

Asymptotic lines on a surface in compression are also the directions of zero membrane stress. This means that structural ribs along asymptotic lines carry load purely in bending, not in membrane action — which is structurally inefficient. For structural facades, principal stress lines (see Section 7) are preferred over asymptotic lines.

However, for non-structural cladding on negative-curvature surfaces, asymptotic networks provide excellent panelization because the panels are naturally close to planar.

## 7. Principal Stress Line Networks for Structural Facades

When the facade itself carries structural loads (self-weight, wind, seismic), the panel and mullion layout should follow the principal stress directions to maximize structural efficiency.

### 7.1 Principal Stress Lines

Under a given loading condition, every point on the facade surface experiences a stress state described by two principal stresses (sigma1, sigma2) and their orthogonal directions (e1, e2). Integrating the principal stress direction field produces two families of curves: the principal stress lines.

**Properties**:
- Along a principal stress line, the shear stress is zero. This is the direction of maximum structural efficiency for a beam or rib — all the material contributes to resisting the dominant stress.
- Mullions placed along principal stress lines experience axial force and bending but no torsion, simplifying connection design.
- The principal stress network is orthogonal (the two families are perpendicular at every point), producing quad panels with right-angle corners — aesthetically regular and structurally efficient.

### 7.2 Computation

1. Build a structural model of the facade surface (shell elements in FEA software: SAP2000, ANSYS, Abaqus, or Karamba3D).
2. Apply design loads (dead load, wind load, seismic, thermal).
3. Extract principal stress magnitudes and directions at each element centroid.
4. Integrate the principal stress direction field using an ODE solver (Runge-Kutta 4th order) to produce stress lines.
5. Select a subset of stress lines at regular intervals to form the structural grid.
6. The intersection of the two families defines the panel boundaries and mullion layout.

**Challenges**:
- Principal stress directions are load-dependent. Different load combinations produce different networks. The designer must choose a dominant load case (typically dead load + wind for facades) or develop a compromise network.
- At points where sigma1 = sigma2 (isotropic stress), the principal directions are undefined (degenerate point). The network must be handled specially at these points — typically by inserting a singular panel (triangle or pentagon).

### 7.3 Case Applications

- **Gridshell structures**: The Mannheim Multihalle (1975) and the Great Court roof of the British Museum (2000, Foster + Partners) use quad grids aligned with principal stress directions for structural efficiency.
- **Diagrid towers**: The Hearst Tower (Foster + Partners, 2006) uses a diagrid aligned with principal stress lines under combined gravity and wind loading, reducing steel tonnage by 20% compared to a conventional frame.
- **Free-form structural skins**: The Heydar Aliyev Center's structural concrete shell has reinforcement aligned with principal stress directions, and the exterior cladding grid follows the same network.

## 8. Level-Set Based Rationalization

Level-set methods represent the design surface implicitly as the zero-level set of a scalar function F(x,y,z) = 0. Rationalization in this framework involves modifying F to achieve constructibility targets.

### 8.1 Method

1. Represent the design surface as a signed distance field F(x,y,z). Points on the surface satisfy F = 0.
2. Define a target surface class (e.g., union of planar patches, union of cylinders, collection of developable strips).
3. Evolve F using a PDE that drives the surface toward the target class while maintaining proximity to the original design:
   dF/dt = -v_rationalization - lambda * (F - F_original)
   Where v_rationalization is a velocity field that flattens curvature (for planarization) or aligns rulings (for developability), and lambda controls the fidelity to the original design.
4. Extract the rationalized surface as the zero level set of the converged F.

### 8.2 Applications

- **Planarization of free-form surfaces**: Drive Gaussian curvature toward zero locally to create developable patches.
- **Cylindrical approximation**: Drive the surface toward the nearest cylinder in each patch region.
- **Sphere-patch approximation**: Approximate the surface as a collection of spherical caps (constant positive curvature patches). Each cap can be fabricated as a mold-pressed piece with high repetition.

### 8.3 Advantages

- Topology changes (splitting a surface into separate pieces, merging patches) are handled naturally in the level-set framework.
- The method is resolution-independent: the same algorithm works at any mesh density.
- Complex boolean operations (subtracting window openings, adding connection bosses) are simple in the implicit representation.

## 9. Cost Functions for Rationalization Optimization

The total facade cost C_total can be modeled as a function of the rationalization parameters, allowing direct optimization for minimum cost.

### 9.1 Cost Model

C_total = C_panels + C_framing + C_connections + C_molds + C_erection

Where:
- C_panels = sum over all panels of c_panel(type_i, area_i, curvature_i)
- C_framing = sum over all mullion segments of c_mullion(length_i, section_i)
- C_connections = sum over all nodes of c_node(complexity_i)
- C_molds = N_unique_geometries * c_mold(complexity)
- C_erection = f(panel_weight, access_difficulty, unique_count)

### 9.2 Panel Cost Function

| Panel Type | Base Cost ($/m²) | Curvature Multiplier | Complexity Multiplier |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|-----------------------|
| Flat glass (IGU) | $150-300 | 1.0 | 1.0 |
| Cold-bent glass | $200-400 | 1.0 + 0.5/R (R in meters) | 1.2 |
| Hot-bent glass | $400-800 | 1.0 + 2.0/R | 1.5 + 0.1 * (1/unique_count_in_family) |
| Flat metal | $80-200 | 1.0 | 1.0 |
| Formed metal | $120-350 | 1.0 + 1.0/R | 1.3 |
| GFRC | $200-500 | 1.0 + 0.5/R | 1.0 + $1000/count_per_mold |

### 9.3 Node Complexity Cost

| Node Type | Cost ($) | When to Use |
|-----------|---------|-------------|
| Orthogonal 4-way | $50-100 | Regular quad mesh, flat panels |
| Non-orthogonal 4-way | $80-200 | Rotated quad mesh, flat panels |
| Torsion-free 4-way | $100-250 | Conical mesh, flat panels |
| Twisted 4-way | $200-500 | General PQ mesh, requires custom connector |
| 3-way (hex mesh) | $60-150 | Triangular or hex mesh |
| 6-way (tri mesh) | $150-400 | Triangulated mesh — complex intersection |
| Custom sculptural | $500-5000 | Free-form, CNC-milled or cast |

### 9.4 Optimization Objective

Minimize C_total subject to:
- Surface deviation from design < delta_max (aesthetic fidelity)
- Panel planarity deviation < 2 mm (fabrication constraint)
- Edge length variation within families < tolerance (clustering constraint)
- Structural adequacy (stress < allowable at all points)
- Minimum panel size > fabrication minimum (typically > 300 x 300 mm)
- Maximum panel size < transport/handling maximum (typically < 3.5 x 6.0 m)
- Maximum panel weight < crane/manual handling capacity (typically < 200 kg for crane, < 25 kg for manual)

## 10. Comparison of Rationalization Approaches by Surface Type

### 10.1 Single-Curved Surfaces (K ≈ 0)

| Approach | Suitability | Panel Type | Cost | Notes |
|----------|------------|-----------|------|-------|
| UV subdivision | Excellent | Flat (naturally planar) | Low | Best approach — panels are inherently planar |
| Strip decomposition | Good | Developable strips | Low | Unnecessary — UV subdivision already works |
| Conical mesh | Overkill | Flat | Medium | Not needed for single-curved surfaces |

**Recommendation**: Use UV subdivision with cold-bent glass if curvature is tight, flat glass if curvature is gentle (panel chord-to-arc deviation < 2 mm).

### 10.2 Double-Curved Surfaces, Low Curvature (0 < |K| < 0.01)

| Approach | Suitability | Panel Type | Cost | Notes |
|----------|------------|-----------|------|-------|
| PQ mesh (conjugate net) | Excellent | Flat quads | Low-Medium | Best balance of cost and quality |
| Conical mesh | Excellent | Flat quads + torsion-free nodes | Medium | Best if multi-layer assembly needed |
| Strip decomposition | Good | Developable strips | Low | Good for metal cladding |
| Triangulation | Adequate | Flat triangles | Medium | Higher edge count, less elegant |
| Cold-bent quads | Good | Warped quads (cold-bent) | Medium | If PQ planarity is not achievable |

**Recommendation**: PQ mesh or conical mesh for glass facades; strip decomposition for metal cladding.

### 10.3 Double-Curved Surfaces, High Curvature (|K| > 0.01)

| Approach | Suitability | Panel Type | Cost | Notes |
|----------|------------|-----------|------|-------|
| Fine PQ mesh | Possible | Small flat quads | Medium-High | Many panels needed for planarity |
| Triangulation | Good | Flat triangles | High (framing) | Guarantees planarity at any curvature |
| Hot-bent glass | Good | Double-curved | Very High | Beautiful but expensive |
| GFRC/FRP panels | Good | Molded double-curved | High | Mold cost amortized over family |
| Cold-bent over frames | Good | Warped quads | Medium-High | If stress limits allow |
| Geodesic network + tri | Good | Flat triangles | High | Good for dome-like surfaces |

**Recommendation**: Depends on budget. For prestige projects: hot-bent glass or molded panels with aggressive clustering. For cost-constrained projects: fine PQ mesh or triangulation.

### 10.4 Negative Curvature Surfaces (K < 0)

| Approach | Suitability | Panel Type | Cost | Notes |
|----------|------------|-----------|------|-------|
| Asymptotic network | Excellent | Nearly-planar quads | Low-Medium | Exploits the surface geometry |
| PQ mesh (conjugate net) | Good | Flat quads | Medium | General approach, always works |
| Ruled surface strips | Excellent | Ruled/developable | Low | Two ruling families available |
| Conical mesh | Good | Flat quads + offsets | Medium | If multi-layer needed |

**Recommendation**: Asymptotic line network for cladding (exploits the natural straight-line directions on the surface); PQ mesh for glazing.

### 10.5 Free-Form Surfaces (Mixed Curvature)

| Approach | Suitability | Panel Type | Cost | Notes |
|----------|------------|-----------|------|-------|
| Hybrid rationalization | Best | Mixed (flat + curved) | Variable | Different strategy per curvature zone |
| Global PQ optimization | Good | Flat quads | Medium-High | Uniform approach, easier to manage |
| Triangulation | Reliable | Flat triangles | High | Always works, never optimal |
| Level-set rationalization | Emerging | Zone-dependent | Variable | Research-grade, very flexible |

**Recommendation**: Divide the surface into zones by Gaussian curvature. Apply the best strategy per zone. Use transition panels (typically triangles or pentagons) at zone boundaries where the mesh topology changes.
